Meta Renting Google's AI Chips

Meta is paying Google to use their custom AI chips. Yes, competitors are now sharing infrastructure.

Glossary

15 terms

DOGE

Not the crypto. The Department of Government Efficiency, Elon Musk's initiative to cut federal spending. It's the controversial project to slash government costs and employees. (Yes, the name is a meme reference. Yes, that was intentional.)

LLM

Large Language Model. The technology behind ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, and every other AI chatbot. Trained on enormous amounts of text, they predict the next word — and when you do that really well at massive scale, you get something that can write code, answer questions, and hold a conversation.

TPU

Tensor Processing Unit. Google's custom AI chips, competing with NVIDIA's GPUs. Google built these specifically for training AI models, giving them more efficiency for AI workloads. Meta just signed a deal to rent them — which is how desperate the AI chip crunch has gotten.

EUV

Extreme Ultraviolet Lithography. The insanely complex technology used to print the tiny circuits on advanced computer chips. Only ASML makes the machines that do this. They cost $200+ million each, weigh as much as two commercial aircraft, and require 100,000 parts. Everything advanced in AI runs on chips made with these machines.

Forward PE

Price-to-Earnings ratio based on future expected earnings. If a stock trades at 20x forward PE, you're paying $20 for every $1 the company is expected to earn next year. Lower = potentially cheaper. Higher = either expensive or the market expects big growth. NVIDIA at 19x looks cheap compared to Costco at 50x — but only if NVIDIA keeps growing.

Alignment

Training AI to behave according to human values. Sounds simple. It's not. "Alignment" is the field of trying to make AI do what you actually want it to do, not just what you literally told it to do. Different AI companies have very different ideas about what "good behavior" means — which is why some AI feels "preachy" and others feel "edgy." It's also the thing that keeps AI safety researchers up at night.

Frontier Model

The most powerful, cutting-edge AI models — think GPT-4o, Claude Opus, Gemini Ultra, and whatever DeepSeek just released. "Frontier" means it's at the absolute edge of what AI can currently do. These are the models that require massive compute, cost millions to train, and regularly get called either "the most dangerous technology ever made" or "just autocomplete." Depends who you ask.

Agentic AI

AI that can take actions on its own — browse the web, write and run code, book meetings, send emails — not just answer questions in a chat window. The "agent" can break a task into steps and execute them autonomously. This is the next big AI frontier: AI that does things for you, not just talks to you. Also the thing that sounds either extremely helpful or extremely terrifying depending on your mood.

xAI

Elon Musk's AI company, founded in 2023 after he left OpenAI's board. Makes Grok, the chatbot built into X (Twitter). Was valued at $230B, then just merged with SpaceX. Musk's pitch: "maximum truth-seeking AI" — which in practice means it's less filtered than competitors. Whether that's a feature or a bug is a matter of hot debate.

Open vs Closed Source

Whether AI model code is publicly available (open source, like Meta's Llama or DeepSeek) or kept secret (closed source, like OpenAI's GPT or Anthropic's Claude). Open source lets anyone download, run, and modify the model. It democratizes AI but also means anyone can use it for anything, including bad things. This debate is huge in AI right now — more contested than whether pineapple belongs on pizza.

GPU

Graphics Processing Unit. Originally made for gaming and rendering visuals, GPUs turned out to be perfect for AI training because they can do millions of math operations in parallel. NVIDIA dominates this market so completely that their chips have become the de facto currency of the AI gold rush. NVIDIA's H100 chip costs ~$30,000 each, and AI labs buy them by the thousands.

Capex

Capital Expenditure — big purchases like buildings, machines, and infrastructure. In AI context: data centers, servers, and chips. Big tech (Google, Meta, Microsoft, Amazon) spent $260 billion on AI infrastructure in 2025 and is planning to spend $600 billion in 2026. These are the biggest corporate spending announcements in history. When your spouse says "capex cycle," they mean: companies are spending insane amounts of money on AI hardware.

Series B

A stage of startup funding. Companies go: Seed → Series A → Series B → Series C, etc. Each round they raise more money at a higher valuation. Series B used to mean $10–50M. In AI-land 2025-2026, it means $100M minimum, often $500M+. It's like inflation, but for startup ambition. OpenAI's $110B "round" would technically be many normal funding stages compressed into one eyebrow-raising number.

IPO

Initial Public Offering — when a private company "goes public" by selling shares on a stock exchange for the first time. It's how early investors and employees cash out, and how companies raise money from everyday investors. When your spouse says SpaceX is "planning an IPO," they mean the company is getting ready to let the public buy in — and it would probably be one of the biggest stock market events in years.

Market Cap

Market Capitalization — the total value of all a company's shares combined. Calculated as: share price × number of shares. It's the most common way to measure "how big" a company is. Apple and NVIDIA both trade above $3 trillion. For comparison, the entire GDP of the UK is about $3.1 trillion. Your spouse mentioning "market cap" means they're talking about how much a company is worth according to the stock market — which may or may not reflect reality.
